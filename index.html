Getting Started in 4 Steps

Up and running in under five minutes — no downloads, no setup.

1

Open Claude

Go to claude.ai in any browser. Works on your phone, tablet, or computer.

2

Sign in or create a free account

Use your work email if Suunto provides a team plan, otherwise a free personal account works great to start.

3

Type your request in the chat box

This is called a prompt. Write what you need as if texting a knowledgeable colleague. No special commands needed.

4

Read it — then refine it

If the first answer isn't right, just say: "Make it shorter," "More formal please," or "Add a line about the new launch." You're always in control.

Chapter 4

Ready-to-Use Prompt Templates

Copy any template, fill in the highlighted parts, paste into Claude, and hit send.

📧 Follow up after a buyer meeting
You are an Account Manager at Suunto UK (sports watches, dive computers, outdoor gear). I just finished a meeting with a retail buyer and need to send a follow-up email today. Buyer: [Name and role, e.g. Head of Buying, Cotswold Outdoor] Account: [Retailer name] What we discussed: "[Paste your rough notes — products covered, pricing, objections, next steps]" Please draft a warm, professional follow-up email that: - Thanks them for their time - Recaps the 2–3 key points we agreed - States clear next steps and owners - Sets up the next touchpoint Keep it under 150 words. Confident but never pushy.
After Claude replies: "Make it warmer" or "Add a line about the Suunto Race Q4 launch."
📋 Turn meeting notes into a clean recap
Please turn my rough notes from a buyer meeting into a clean internal recap I can paste into our CRM and share with the UK Sales team. My rough notes: "[Paste anything — bullet points, voice-note transcript, scribbled thoughts]" Format the output as: • Account & attendees: • Headline (one sentence — what's the most important thing?): • Discussion points: • Buyer concerns / objections: • Commitments made (by us and by them): • Next steps & owners: • Follow-up due:
Run this in the car-park after a customer visit while it's all still fresh.
📊 Prep a Quarterly Business Review
I'm an Account Manager at Suunto UK preparing a Quarterly Business Review with one of my retail partners. Account: [Retailer name, e.g. Snow + Rock, 12 UK stores] Last quarter highlights: [YoY growth %, hero products, missed targets, activations run] Looking ahead: [Planned launches, range changes, market context] Please give me: 1. A suggested QBR agenda (45 min meeting) 2. The 3 most important talking points I should land 3. Two open questions to ask the buyer 4. One honest risk I should flag proactively before they raise it
A great QBR is a conversation, not a presentation. The honest risk point earns trust every time.
🚀 Build a sell-in pitch for a new product
I'm pitching a new Suunto product into one of my UK retail accounts. Please help me build a compelling sell-in story. Product: [e.g. Suunto Race S, new colourway launching Q1] Retailer: [Account name and type — e.g. Up & Running, 25 stores, run specialists] Key facts: [RRP, trade margin, USP, target customer] Please give me: 1. A one-sentence pitch ("In one breath, here's why you should stock this…") 2. Three reasons this works for THIS retailer's customer base 3. The expected objection and how I'd respond to it 4. A suggested opening order quantity, with the rationale UK retail context. British English spelling.
Tell Claude the retailer's customer profile — the pitch lands much better when tailored.
📈 Justify a monthly demand forecast
I need to submit my monthly demand forecast for one of my UK accounts and write a short rationale that holds up to scrutiny from supply & finance. Account: [Retailer name] Forecast: [e.g. 420 units of Suunto Vertical for September, up 18% on last year] Drivers I'm seeing: [New stores opening, planned activation, seasonal demand, competitor move, etc.] Please write a clear 5–8 line rationale that: - Explains what's driving the number - Acknowledges one downside risk honestly - Shows the assumption I'd revise the forecast against (the "tripwire") Clean business tone — no fluff.
The honest downside risk is what gets your forecast trusted next time.
🤝 Rehearse a tough negotiation
I have a negotiation coming up with a retail partner and I want to rehearse before the meeting. The situation: "[Describe what's at stake — e.g. buyer wants 2 extra margin points to keep stocking Suunto, or wants exclusivity on a new colourway, or is threatening to delist]" What I can flex on: [e.g. marketing co-funding, POS support, exclusive timing window, training days] What I cannot move on: [e.g. headline trade margin, MAP pricing] Please help me: 1. List the three most likely arguments the buyer will use 2. Give me a strong, calm response to each 3. Suggest a creative "trade" — what I could offer instead of what they're asking for 4. Write one opening line that sets a collaborative tone
Always know your walk-away point before the meeting. Claude is rehearsal — you decide what to offer.
🏪 Plan a retail staff training session
I'm running an in-store training session for the staff of a Suunto retail partner. The goal is to lift their confidence in selling Suunto so they recommend us more often. Retailer: [Store name, type and staff size — e.g. flagship Snow + Rock store, 8 staff] Duration: [e.g. 30 minutes between shifts] Focus product: [e.g. Suunto Race S, or the dive range, or the full sports watch line] Please give me: 1. A minute-by-minute agenda 2. The three "must-land" talking points 3. Two demo moments that get hands on the product 4. A simple cheat-sheet with the 3 most common customer questions and how to answer them Friendly, energetic tone. UK retail context.
Staff who feel confident sell more. Bring a demo unit and let them play with it.
📝 Write a weekly update for the UK team
Please turn my rough notes from the week into a clean update for the UK Sales & Marketing team. Tight, scannable, no fluff. My notes: "[Everything from the week — accounts visited, deals progressed, blockers, wins, risks]" Format as: • Headline of the week (one sentence): • Wins: • Risks & blockers (with the help I need): • Forecast movement vs plan: • Next week's priorities (top 3): UK English spelling. Confident but honest tone — flag risks early, don't dress them up.
Managers love updates that flag risks early. It builds trust and gets you help before the wheels come off.

Chapter 5

Tips for Better Results

Small habits that make a big difference in the quality of Claude's answers.

🎯

Give Claude a role

Start with "You are a Suunto Customer Success specialist…" — it shapes the whole tone.

📏

Set a word limit

Say "under 100 words" or "3 bullet points" so the output fits exactly where you need it.

🔁

Ask for a rewrite

"Try again but warmer" or "Less formal please" — Claude never minds being asked again.

🧩

Give more context

The product, the issue, what's been tried — the more Claude knows, the better the answer.

👁️

Always review first

Claude is fast but not infallible. You are the expert — read it over before sending.

🔒

Protect customer data

Replace real names, emails, and order numbers with [Customer] or [#ID] if needed.

Do's & Don'ts

Keep these in mind every time you use Claude at work.

✅ Do

  • Give the full context — account, history, what's at stake
  • Tell Claude who the audience is (buyer, internal team, manager)
  • Iterate — ask follow-ups to sharpen the output
  • Use it to rehearse difficult conversations before the call
  • Always edit the output before sending or pasting into CRM
  • Use it to save time on the writing, not on the thinking

❌ Don't

  • Send Claude's draft word-for-word — your relationships are personal
  • Paste passwords, contract figures, or anything truly confidential
  • Treat Claude's commercial suggestions as company policy
  • Expect it to know real-time stock, pricing, or order status
  • Rely on it for the latest Suunto launch dates or NDA info
  • Use it to commit to buyers on Suunto's behalf — that's still your call
